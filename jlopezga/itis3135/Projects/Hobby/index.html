What is paragliding?

Paragliding is an exhilarating aerial sport where pilots soar through the sky suspended beneath a lightweight, free-flying wing called a paraglider. The wing, made of ripstop nylon, maintains its shape through the pressure of air entering through vents in the leading edge, while the pilot sits in a harness below, controlling direction and speed through brake lines and weight shift. Launches typically occur from hills or mountains, where pilots can catch rising air currents called thermals to gain altitude and stay airborne for hours.

Unlike powered aircraft, paragliding relies entirely on natural air movements and the pilot's skill to achieve lift and maintain flight. Experienced paragliders can travel hundreds of kilometers by strategically using thermal updrafts, ridge lift, and wind patterns. The sport offers an unparalleled sense of freedom and a bird's-eye perspective of the landscape below, though it requires careful weather assessment, thorough training, and a deep understanding of aerodynamics and meteorology to practice safely.

When to paraglide?

The best conditions for paragliding typically occur during the morning and early evening hours when thermal activity is moderate and winds are stable. Ideal conditions include:

  • Wind speeds between 5-15 mph (8-24 km/h)
  • Clear visibility with minimal cloud cover
  • No approaching storms or unstable weather
  • Spring and summer months generally offer the best conditions
  • Early morning flights often provide the smoothest air

Where to paraglide?

Paragliding can be done in many locations worldwide, but the best sites offer specific geographical and weather characteristics:

  • Mountains and hills with clear launch areas facing prevailing winds
  • Coastal areas with consistent sea breezes
  • Popular destinations include:
    • The Alps (Switzerland, France, Italy)
    • Oludeniz, Turkey
    • Chamonix, France
    • California's coastal ranges
    • Hawaii's tropical coastlines

How to paraglide?

Learning to paraglide requires proper training and certification through a licensed school. The process typically involves:

  • Ground handling training (learning to control the wing on the ground)
  • Basic theory courses covering aerodynamics and weather
  • Small-height training flights with radio guidance
  • Progressive advancement to higher flights
  • Certification requirements (usually 25-40 flights minimum)
  • Safety and emergency procedure training
